Stuff, Rights, and Promises

An observation about what crosses the boundary

Look back at the arrows you just built. In some exchanges, tangible things — stuff — cross the boundary in both directions: a supplier delivers goods, the entity pays cash. But in many exchanges, what crosses in one direction is not stuff at all. It is a right or a promise.

An owner puts resources in. What goes out? Not stuff — a right to control the entity and a residual claim on whatever is left. A lender puts cash in. What goes out? A promise to repay, with interest, on a schedule. A customer receives goods but might not pay immediately — what comes in is a promise to pay later (accounts receivable). The first step when something crosses the boundary is to analyze it: dig out the information in the transaction. What actually changed hands? Was it stuff, a right, or a promise? It does not matter whether there was a formal contract or a handshake — the analytical task is the same.

This habit of looking at each exchange and asking what actually crossed, in each direction is the analytical skill that the rest of the course will build on. The notation used in journal entries (Module 8) is simply a way to formalize it. For now, practice the habit with the prompts below.
