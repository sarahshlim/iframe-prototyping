Every accounting system begins with a choice: what is the entity? A corporation, a partnership, a nonprofit, a government agency, a trust. The entity is not just a name — it is a boundary. Everything inside the boundary is what the system accounts for. Everything outside is a counterparty — someone the entity exchanges things with.

These exchanges — transactions — are where accounting started and remain its central core. When we observe a transaction, what do we record? The information in it: what was exchanged, how much, between whom, when, on what terms. The earliest accounting records — clay tablets, medieval ledgers — were doing exactly this: capturing the information in exchanges so it could be consulted later. Everything else the system does is built on top of that transaction-information base.

The boundary choice seems simple when there is one company. But consider a parent corporation that owns three subsidiaries. Is the entity the parent alone? Each subsidiary separately? Or the entire group consolidated into one?

Each choice produces a different picture. If you draw the boundary around a single subsidiary, transactions between that subsidiary and its parent show up as external transactions — arrows crossing the boundary. If you draw the boundary around the entire consolidated group, those same transactions disappear — they are internal, and the system does not report on them.

This is not a theoretical curiosity. Under US GAAP, companies must consolidate subsidiaries they control, and the rules for determining control have real consequences. Before the 2008 financial crisis, some firms used structures called variable interest entities to keep certain obligations outside the consolidation boundary — off the balance sheet. The boundary choice determined what users could see.

The entity boundary is itself an instance choice. Different rule sets draw it differently, and where they draw it shapes the entire picture the system produces.

Every arrow on the diagram represents a transaction between the entity and a counterparty. The counterparty — a supplier, a lender, an employee — often has its own accounting system, recording the same transaction from the other side of the boundary. You might expect the two records to match. They do not have to.

The two entities may be operating under different instances of the architecture, with different recognition rules and different timing conventions. A supplier may recognize revenue when goods ship; the entity may not recognize the expense until the goods are consumed. A lender’s books may show an asset (loan receivable) using one interest accrual method; the entity’s books may show the corresponding liability using a different one. Same transaction, same cash flows, different numbers at any given point in time.

It goes further than timing. The two sides may not even agree on whether a transaction has occurred. A customer disputes an invoice — the entity has recorded a receivable, but the customer has not recorded a payable. One side sees an obligation; the other does not. This is normal, not a failure. Each entity’s system records what its own conventions require, from its own side of the boundary. Reconciliation — the process of comparing the two sides and working out the differences — is an entire practice built around this fact.
